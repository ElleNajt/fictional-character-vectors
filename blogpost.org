* Introduction

This analysis stress-tests the methodology from [[https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.10387][Lu et al. (2026), "The Assistant Axis"]] by applying it to fictional characters with minimal prompts.

Lu et al. claim there's a coherent "persona space" - a low-dimensional subspace where personas live, with a principal component (the "persona axis") that behaves similarly across models. They define personas using 275 role descriptions (e.g., "You are a helpful assistant who is also a therapist").

We stress-test this by asking: *does Lu's persona space generalize to fictional characters?*

Specifically:
1. How much variance of fictional characters is captured by Lu's 275-role space?
2. What structure exists in the residual (orthogonal to Lu's space)?
3. How sensitive are character vectors to which questions are asked?
4. Can we interpret what PCA axes *mean* using the model's own unembedding matrix?

Along the way, we find that modern franchises share a near-identical principal axis (cosine ~0.85) while mythology operates in an orthogonal subspace. We also find that projecting the LM head onto PCA directions --- a logit lens on the axes themselves --- reveals interpretable semantics: the dominant axis across nearly every universe is a *register* dimension (formal/archaic vs. colloquial/casual), and Lu's role PCA encodes dimensions like groundedness, warmth, and calculation (see [[*Logit Lens: What Do the Axes Mean in Token Space?][logit lens]]).

** Method

We extract activations from Qwen3-32B for 1,268 fictional characters (1,346 role files deduplicated to remove characters appearing under multiple source lists) using the same 240 questions as Lu et al.

Characters were gathered from two sources: a hand-curated list of ~370 culturally diverse characters (drawn from Wikipedia lists of major characters, literary surveys, and mythology references), supplemented by Wikidata-ranked additions for popular contemporary characters. We then added comprehensive character lists for several well-known universes (Harry Potter, Star Wars, LOTR, Marvel, Game of Thrones, Naruto, Shakespeare, and five mythology traditions: Greek, Chinese, Hindu, Norse, and Egyptian).

** Prompting Strategy

Role prompts are minimal: just the character name and source (e.g., "You are Harry Potter from Harry Potter."). We intentionally omit character descriptions - Lu gave short descriptions, but we let the model's own representation of the character emerge.

This is a harder test: can Lu's generic roles capture characters the model already knows?

#+begin_src python
# PCA on residuals
resid_pca = SkPCA(n_components=10)
resid_transformed = resid_pca.fit_transform(residuals)

# How much of each residual PC is explained by universe?
sources = [name.split('__')[0] for name in char_names]
unique_sources = list(set(sources))

rows = []
for pc_idx in range(5):
    scores = resid_transformed[:, pc_idx]
    var_pct = resid_pca.explained_variance_ratio_[pc_idx] * 100
    
    grand_mean = np.mean(scores)
    ss_total = np.sum((scores - grand_mean) ** 2)
    ss_between = sum(
        np.sum([s == src for s in sources]) * (np.mean(scores[[s == src for s in sources]]) - grand_mean) ** 2
        for src in unique_sources
    )
    eta_sq = ss_between / ss_total
    rows.append({'PC': f'PC{pc_idx+1}', 'Variance': f'{var_pct:.1f}%', 'From Universe (η²)': f'{eta_sq:.0%}'})

df = pd.DataFrame(rows).set_index('PC')
print(df)

rows = []

Characters with large residuals are the *distinctive* ones: protagonists (Luke, Frodo), iconic objects (the Sorting Hat), and characters whose identity is deeply tied to their universe (R2-D2, Gollum). The more a character is "just a villain" or "just a monster," the better Lu's generic roles explain them. The more a character is *specifically themselves*, the more they escape Lu's space.

* Understanding the Residual: Directions vs Character Rankings

An initially confusing finding: after projecting out Lu's space (which captures 70% of within-universe variance), character rankings on Residual PC1 still correlate ~0.9 with rankings on Original PC1. Does this mean the residual is just recapturing what Lu already had?

No. The distinction between *direction cosines* (how aligned two axes are) and *ranking correlations* (how similarly they order characters) resolves the confusion:

#+begin_src python
def cosine(a, b):
    return np.dot(a, b) / (np.linalg.norm(a) * np.linalg.norm(b))

The key insight: *direction cosines* and *ranking correlations* tell different stories.

- Lu-Reconstructed PC1 direction aligns ~0.9 with Original PC1 - Lu captures the axis well
- Residual PC1 direction is nearly orthogonal to Original (~0.3-0.5)
- But residual character rankings still correlate ~0.9 with original rankings

Why? Extreme characters (Tom Riddle, Darth Vader, Galadriel) are extreme in *multiple* directions. The residual PC1 is a different axis, but it still separates the same characters because those characters spread out along a diagonal that has components in both Lu's space and orthogonal to it.

To summarize this more cleanly, we measure subspace alignment: given the top-k PCs of the original activations and top-k PCs of the residual, compute the mean cosine of the principal angles between the two subspaces.

#+begin_src python
from scipy.linalg import svdvals

def subspace_similarity(pca1, pca2, k=3):
    """Mean cosine of principal angles between top-k PC subspaces."""
    U1 = pca1.components_[:k]
    U2 = pca2.components_[:k]
    cos_angles = svdvals(U1 @ U2.T)
    return cos_angles.mean()

*** Question Attributions Along Residual PC1

Each character's vector is the mean of its 240 per-question activations (after scaling and removing the role-space component):

$$\vec{c} = \frac{1}{240} \sum_{q=1}^{240} \vec{r}_q$$

where $\vec{r}_q$ is the residual activation for question $q$. The character's score on a PC axis is the dot product with the PC direction $\vec{d}$:

$$\text{score} = \vec{c} \cdot \vec{d} = \frac{1}{240} \sum_{q=1}^{240} \vec{r}_q \cdot \vec{d}$$

So each question's contribution to the PC score is just $a_q = \vec{r}_q \cdot \vec{d}$ -- the projection of that question's residual onto the PC direction. This is an exact additive decomposition (no approximation): the 240 projections sum to 240 times the PC score.

To find the most informative questions, we compute $a_q$ for every character in a universe and take the cross-character variance $\text{Var}_c(a_q)$. Questions with high variance are the ones where characters diverge most along the axis -- these are the questions to read for interpreting what the PC means.

The same decomposition works at the individual character level: a character's 240 projections $a_q$ are its per-question "SHAP values" along the PC. Sorting them shows which questions push that specific character toward the high or low end of the axis.

* Logit Lens: What Do the Axes Mean in Token Space?

Question attribution showed that no small set of questions drives a character's position on a PCA axis --- the signal is diffuse across all 240 questions (effective N ~200, top 5 questions < 5% of score). So we can't interpret axes by reading "the questions that matter most."

Instead, we can ask the model directly. At layer 32, activations will eventually be decoded into next-token logits via the LM head (unembedding matrix). Projecting each vocabulary token's embedding onto a PCA direction tells us which tokens that direction "points toward" --- a logit lens on the PCA axis itself.

Concretely: take a PCA direction $d$ in activation space, project the LM head weight matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{|V| \times 5120}$ onto it, and rank tokens by $W \cdot d$. No probing, no training --- just a dot product between vocabulary embeddings and the axis.

** What this tells us

#+begin_quote
*Claude's reading:* The logit lens confirms that character PCA axes are not arbitrary statistical artifacts. They correspond to directions the model uses to organize its vocabulary --- the same directions that will eventually produce different next-token distributions when the model talks about or as different characters.

The dominant axis across nearly every universe is a *register* dimension: formal/archaic/mythic vs. colloquial/casual/grounded. This makes sense --- when the model roleplays as Voldemort, it shifts its entire token distribution toward formal, archaic language; as Ron Weasley, toward casual English. The PCA axis captures exactly this shift.

However, the *residual* PCA directions (after removing Lu's role space) do not yield interpretable logit lens results --- the top tokens are code fragments, random subwords, and semantically incoherent Chinese. This is a striking asymmetry: Lu's PCA directions have clean token-level semantics, but the residual directions don't. Whatever the residual encodes, it doesn't reduce to vocabulary shifts --- it likely lives in distributed patterns across higher-order response structure (see [[*Interpreting Residual PCs: Response Diffing][response diffing]]).
#+end_quote

** The pattern

#+begin_quote
*Claude's reading:* The residual PC1 is *not* a single universal axis --- its sign and meaning vary by universe. But a consistent theme emerges: it separates *characters who engage as specific, relatable individuals* from *characters who speak as archetypal forces or abstract authorities*.

The most discriminating features are:

1. *First-person usage* (I/me/my): Grounded characters use first-person heavily, speaking from personal experience. Cosmic/archetypal characters use impersonal, universal language.
2. *Philosophical vocabulary*: Abstract entities invoke "existence," "truth," "cosmic," "fate." Grounded characters talk about specific situations and practical advice.
3. *Casual markers*: Relatable characters use "yeah," "gonna," "hey," "like." Archetypal characters avoid colloquial language entirely.

** What the residual encodes

#+begin_quote
*Claude's reading:* The residual PC1 captures something we might call *narrative individuation*: how much the model treats a character as a specific person with personal experiences, relationships, and practical knowledge, versus as an archetype, force of nature, or philosophical authority.

This sounds similar to Lu et al.'s own characterization of their PC1 as "deviation from the Assistant persona" --- with fantastical/dramatic roles (bard, ghost, trickster) at one end and analytical/assistant-like roles (evaluator, reviewer, consultant) at the other. And indeed, residual PC1 rankings correlate ~0.9 with original PC1 rankings across most universes, despite the directions being geometrically orthogonal (cosine ~0.3--0.5).

This is the interpretive ambiguity flagged [[*Understanding the Residual: Directions vs Character Rankings][earlier]]. Either Lu's 275 roles only partially span a single underlying "individuation" dimension (leaving ~40% of its variance in the residual), or the residual captures genuinely different structure that happens to produce similar orderings. We don't have a clean way to distinguish these from the current analysis.

The more parsimonious reading is probably (1): Lu's roles are quite generic (therapist, soldier, bohemian), so it's unsurprising that 275 of them wouldn't fully span the space of how models represent character identity. Fictional characters have richer, more specific identities that push further along the same dimension. The residual would then be the portion of this dimension that generic role descriptions can't reach.

What *is* clear is that the logit lens behaves differently for the residual. Lu's role PCA directions give clean token-level semantics when projected through the LM head --- PC1 maps to register tokens (formal/archaic vs. colloquial), PC3 maps to warmth vs. calculation, and so on. The residual PC directions don't. Projecting residual PC1 through the unembedding matrix produces noisy, uninterpretable token lists.

This is consistent with the parsimonious reading: Lu's roles capture the first-order vocabulary/register component of individuation (the part that shows up as token frequency shifts), while the residual contains the higher-order structural component (response organization, anecdote vs. pronouncement, practical vs. philosophical framing) that doesn't reduce to individual token probabilities. The response diffing and LLM feature coding confirm the residual axis is real and text-observable (adj. R^{2} ~0.77 on held-out characters), but its signature is spread across higher-order textual properties rather than concentrated in any single token dimension.
#+end_quote

** Validating Residual PC Interpretations

The response diffing above is suggestive, but it relies on our own reading of the LLM's comparisons. Can we validate more rigorously that the residual PCs encode text-observable features?

We design a held-out validation experiment with strict separation between discovery and testing:

1. *Feature discovery (odd-ranked characters)*: For each universe and PC, we take the 5 characters with the most extreme positive scores (ranks 1, 3, 5, 7, 9) and the 5 most extreme negative scores (same odd ranks). We show an LLM (Claude Sonnet) their anonymized responses to 10 discriminative questions and ask it to propose 6 features that distinguish the two groups. Features must be observable from text alone --- no universe knowledge.

2. *Feature coding (even-ranked characters)*: We take a *non-overlapping* set: the 10 characters at ranks 2, 4, 6, ..., 20 from each end (20 per universe-PC). A fresh LLM call rates each character on the 6 discovered features (1--5 scale) based on a different sample of 10 questions from the top 50 most discriminative.

3. *Regression*: We regress each character's actual residual PC score against their 6 feature ratings using OLS. High adjusted R^{2} means the LLM-discovered features (from held-out characters) predict PC position well.

The striding ensures zero overlap between the characters used to discover features and those used to validate them. Full code is in =src/analysis/llm_feature_coding.py= and =src/analysis/feature_regression.py=.

** Subspace Alignment (Multiple PCs)

PC1 cosine similarity only measures whether the single most important axis aligns. We can ask a stronger question: do the top-k PC subspaces overlap? Principal angles between subspaces generalize the idea of cosine similarity from single vectors to multi-dimensional subspaces. Given the top-k PCs from two universes, the SVD of their inner product matrix yields cosines of the principal angles -- 1.0 means perfectly aligned subspaces, 0.0 means orthogonal.

#+begin_src python
from scipy.linalg import svdvals
import numpy as np

def subspace_similarity(pca1, pca2, k=3):
    """Mean cosine of principal angles between top-k PC subspaces."""
    U1 = pca1.components_[:k]  # (k, d)
    U2 = pca2.components_[:k]  # (k, d)
    # Cosines of principal angles = singular values of U1 @ U2.T
    cos_angles = svdvals(U1 @ U2.T)
    return cos_angles.mean()

# Compute subspace alignment for k=3
k = 3
sub_alignment = np.zeros((n, n))
for i, u1 in enumerate(universes_list):
    for j, u2 in enumerate(universes_list):
        sub_alignment[i, j] = subspace_similarity(
            universe_pcas[u1]['pca'], universe_pcas[u2]['pca'], k=k
        )

df_sub = pd.DataFrame(sub_alignment, index=universes_list, columns=universes_list).round(2)
