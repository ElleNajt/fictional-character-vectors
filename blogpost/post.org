* Introduction

[[https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.10387][Lu et al. (2026)]] prompt language models with 275 generic roles (therapist, sage, rebel, accountant, ...) and do PCA on the resulting activations. The space is low-dimensional: 4--19 PCs capture 70% of variance (depending on model). PC1 points from role-playing toward default-assistant behavior --- they call this the "Assistant Axis." Their findings generalize across models.

We apply the role space to 1,268 fictional characters in Qwen3-32B --- from Hindu mythology to Naruto --- and ask how much it captures and what it misses. Three questions and what we found:

1. *How much of persona space do 275 generic roles cover?*
   - 75% of fictional character variance overall; 80--85% within modern franchises, 61--72% within mythology/Shakespeare.
   - However, to interpret that we need to know that the fictional character space is low-dimensional (~40 PCs for 90%; within-universe, 3--5 PCs for 70%). So while a completely random 275-dim subspace covers 5% of it, 275 random fictional characters capture 95% (unsurprising in light of 275 >> 40). Even a single universe covers a large part of it: Harry Potter (102 chars) 69%, Greek Mythology (107) 61%, Shakespeare (93) 60%.
   - We attempt to understand the remaining 25%. It turns out to be mostly universe separation; only ~9% is within-universe structure the role space misses.

2. *What do the top PCs encode, and where does the assistant axis live?*
   - Within-universe PC1 is formality/register for modern franchises; correlates with the assistant axis there (rank r = 0.63--0.78).
   - For mythology and Shakespeare, PC1 captures other structure --- but the assistant axis doesn't disappear: it loads on PC2 or PC3 instead (Hindu PC2: 0.79, Greek PC2: 0.63, Chinese PC3: 0.79). The assistant axis signal is always in the top 2--3 PCs; which PC it dominates depends on the character distribution.
   - Geometric directions not aligned across universes (median cosine ~0.13 with role PC1), but modern franchises cluster tightly (HP/Naruto ~0.95).

3. *How sensitive are results to the question battery?*
   - A single random question recovers character rankings at median r > 0.95; five questions get to r > 0.99.
   - The persona signal is in the residual stream regardless of what the model is asked.

The assistant axis finding is the most striking: formality is always present in the top 2--3 PCs, but which PC it dominates depends on the character distribution. Lu et al. show that for a fixed role set, the geometry is consistent across models --- our results show the converse matters too: changing the character distribution changes which directions dominate, even within the same model.

** Definitions

Each character or role is represented by a mean activation vector in 5120-dimensional space (Qwen3-32B layer 32). We work with four types of PC directions, obtained by running PCA on different subsets of these vectors:

- *Role PCs:* PCA on 275 generic role activations (from Lu et al.). Fit once; shared across all analyses.
- *Assistant axis:* The direction from mean role-playing activation to mean default-assistant activation (from Lu et al.'s data). Cosine with role PC1 is ~0.67 --- related but not identical (consistent with Lu et al.'s report of >0.71 at middle layers).
- *Within-universe PCs:* PCA on one universe's characters using their full activations (e.g., PCA on the 102 Harry Potter vectors). Different per universe.
- *Within-universe residual PCs:* PCA on one universe's characters after projecting out the role subspace. Different per universe; orthogonal to the role space by construction.

When the text says "role PC1" vs. "within-universe PC1" vs. "residual PC1," these are three different directions from three different PCA fits.

*Role subspace decomposition.* The 275 role PCs define a subspace. Any direction w decomposes as w = w_{role} + w_{\perp}, where w_{role} is the projection onto the role subspace and w_{\perp} is orthogonal to it. We define:
- =frac_in_role= = ||w_{role}||^{2} / ||w||^{2} --- fraction of the direction inside the role subspace.
- =role_perp_corr= --- correlation between characters' scores on w_{role} and w_{\perp}. High correlation means the two components rank characters the same way.

*Residuals.* For each character activation x, the residual is x minus its projection onto the role subspace. Within-universe residual PCs are PCA on these residuals within a universe.

* Is There a Persona Space?

Before asking how much the role space covers, we check whether fictional character activations are low-dimensional at all.

** How Sensitive Are Results to the Question Battery?

Each character vector is a mean activation over responses to 240 questions. How much does the choice of questions matter? We subsample: pick /k/ random questions (for /k/ = 1, 2, 3, 5, ..., 240), average only those, and correlate the resulting character rankings with the full-battery rankings. We repeat each draw 200 times and report medians across 12 universes.

The role space captures 75% of total fictional character variance. But the 95% baseline needs careful interpretation: fiction variance lives in roughly 40 dimensions (40 PCs for 90%), so 275 random draws from the fiction distribution massively oversample the subspace and will span nearly all of it by linear algebra alone. The 95% is not a finding --- it's a sanity check that the manifold is low-dimensional. The 5.4% null (a random 275-dim subspace of R^{5120}) confirms that the structure is real. The actual finding is the gap: roles capture 75%, not 95%, because they come from a different distribution --- generic personas without universe-specific structure --- and miss directions that fiction has. As the residual decomposition below shows, that 20% gap is mostly universe separation.

Even a single universe's subspace captures a surprising fraction of all 1,268 characters' variance:

But are the principal directions the same? We decompose each within-universe PC into its role-space component and its orthogonal component, then check whether they rank characters the same way:

#+begin_src python
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA as SkPCA

rows = []
for name, prefixes in ALL_UNIVERSES.items():
    idx = get_universe_indices(prefixes)
    if len(idx) < 20:
        continue
    lu_part = chars_centered[idx] @ role_pca.components_.T @ role_pca.components_
    resid_part = residuals[idx]

    n_pcs = min(5, len(idx) - 1)
    within_pca = SkPCA(n_components=n_pcs).fit(chars_centered[idx])

    for pc_i in range(n_pcs):
        w = within_pca.components_[pc_i]
        w_lu = role_pca.components_.T @ (role_pca.components_ @ w)
        w_perp = w - w_lu
        frac_in_lu = np.linalg.norm(w_lu)**2

        lu_scores = lu_part @ w_lu
        perp_scores = resid_part @ w_perp
        if np.std(lu_scores) > 1e-10 and np.std(perp_scores) > 1e-10:
            corr = abs(np.corrcoef(lu_scores, perp_scores)[0, 1])
        else:
            corr = float('nan')

        rows.append({'universe': name, 'PC': pc_i + 1,
                     'frac_in_role': round(frac_in_lu, 2),
                     'role_perp_corr': round(corr, 2),
                     'var_explained': round(within_pca.explained_variance_ratio_[pc_i], 3)})

df = pd.DataFrame(rows)
# Show summary: median across universes for each PC
summary = df.groupby('PC').agg(
    med_frac_in_role=('frac_in_role', 'median'),
    med_role_perp_corr=('role_perp_corr', 'median'),
    med_var_expl=('var_explained', 'median')
).round(3)
print(summary)

For PC1, the test is nearly tautological: if formality dominates within-universe variation (as the LLM labels in the next section suggest), then splitting a formality direction into its role-part and orthogonal-part and finding both correlate with formality is expected. The test is more informative for PC3--5, where the dominant axis is less clear, and =role_perp_corr= remains high (~0.80) even there.

A more direct test: how similar are the actual PC directions? We compute cosine similarities between each universe's within-universe PC1 direction and role PC1, and between the within-universe and residual PC1 directions. We also check whether within-universe PC1 directions align with each other across universes:

# Cosine similarity: within-universe PC directions vs role PCs
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA as SkPCA

def cosine_sim(a, b):
    return np.dot(a, b) / (np.linalg.norm(a) * np.linalg.norm(b))

rows = []
for name, prefixes in ALL_UNIVERSES.items():
    idx = get_universe_indices(prefixes)
    if len(idx) < 20:
        continue

    # Within-universe PCs (on full activations)
    within_pca = SkPCA(n_components=5).fit(chars_centered[idx])

    # Residual PCs (on residuals only)
    resid_pca = SkPCA(n_components=5).fit(residuals[idx])

    for pc_i in range(2):
        w_within = within_pca.components_[pc_i]
        w_resid = resid_pca.components_[pc_i]
        lu_dir = role_pca.components_[pc_i]

        rows.append({
            'Universe': name,
            'PC': pc_i + 1,
            'within_vs_lu': round(abs(cosine_sim(w_within, lu_dir)), 3),
            'within_vs_resid': round(abs(cosine_sim(w_within, w_resid)), 3),
            'w_within': w_within,
            'w_resid': w_resid,
        })

df_cos = pd.DataFrame(rows)
for pc_i in [1, 2]:
    sub = df_cos[df_cos['PC'] == pc_i]
    print(f"\n=== PC{pc_i} cosine similarities (absolute) ===")
    print(sub[['Universe', 'within_vs_lu', 'within_vs_resid']].set_index('Universe'))

    # Cross-universe: do within-universe PC directions align with each other?
    dirs = list(sub['w_within'])
    names = list(sub['Universe'])
    cross = []
    for i in range(len(dirs)):
        for j in range(i+1, len(dirs)):
            cross.append({'pair': f"{names[i]} / {names[j]}",
                          'cosine': round(abs(cosine_sim(dirs[i], dirs[j])), 3)})
    cross_sorted = sorted(cross, key=lambda x: x['cosine'], reverse=True)
    med = np.median([c['cosine'] for c in cross_sorted])
    print(f"  Cross-universe within-PC{pc_i} cosine: median={med:.3f}")
    top5 = ', '.join(f"{c['pair']} ({c['cosine']:.3f})" for c in cross_sorted[:5])
    bot5 = ', '.join(f"{c['pair']} ({c['cosine']:.3f})" for c in cross_sorted[-5:])
    print(f"  Top 5:    {top5}")
    print(f"  Bottom 5: {bot5}")

print(f"\nMedians:")

* What Do the Directions Encode?

The geometric analysis shows the residual is largely redundant with the role space (=role_perp_corr= is high), but can't tell us what semantic content the PCs actually encode --- and the actual PC directions are not aligned across universes, so we can't just read off what they mean from the geometry. The question battery redundancy helps here: since any 5 questions carry the full signal, we can show an LLM a handful of responses from extreme characters and ask what distinguishes them.

We apply this pipeline to three types of PC directions: within-universe PCs (PCA on one universe's full activations), role PCs applied per-universe (projecting each universe's characters onto Lu et al.'s role PC directions and running discovery/regression on those scores), and residual PCs (within-universe PCA after projecting out the role space). The pipeline has three steps with strict separation between discovery and validation:

1. *Feature discovery (odd-ranked characters)*: For each universe and PC, we take the 5 characters with the most extreme positive scores (ranks 1, 3, 5, 7, 9) and the 5 most extreme negative scores (same odd ranks). We show an LLM (Claude Sonnet) their anonymized responses to 10 discriminative questions and ask it to propose 6 features that distinguish the two groups. Features must be observable from text alone --- no universe knowledge.

2. *Feature coding (all remaining characters)*: We code *all* characters in the universe except the 10 used for discovery (typically 34--98 characters depending on universe size). A fresh LLM call rates each character on the 6 discovered features (1--5 scale) based on a random sample of 10 questions.

3. *Regression*: We regress each character's PC score against their 6 feature ratings using OLS. High adjusted R^{2} means the LLM-discovered features (from held-out characters) predict PC position well.

The striding ensures zero overlap between the characters used to discover features and those used to validate them. Full code is in =blogpost/scripts/llm_feature_coding.py= and =blogpost/scripts/feature_regression.py=.

* Conclusion

Persona space is moderately low-dimensional: 3--13 PCs capture 70% of within-universe variance for universes of ~100 characters. The question battery is massively redundant --- even a single random question recovers PC1--5 rankings at median r > 0.95, and five questions get to median r > 0.99.

The 275 generic roles capture ~75% of fictional character variance, though the subspace spanned by 275 random fictional characters captures ~95% --- the subspace is low-dimensional enough that any sample spans it. Of the ~25% residual, most is universe separation; ~9% of total variance is within-universe structure orthogonal to the role space.

The assistant axis signal is always present in the top 2--3 within-universe PCs, but which PC it dominates depends on the character distribution. For modern franchises, it loads on PC1 (rank r = 0.63--0.78); for mythology and Shakespeare, it shifts to PC2 or PC3 (Hindu PC2: 0.79, Greek PC2: 0.63, Chinese PC3: 0.79). Formality dominates PC1 when the character set varies most along formality --- but when other structure dominates (e.g., mortal-vs-divine in mythology), formality gets pushed to a lower PC.

Put differently: the role subspace spans the right region of activation space, but the principal directions within that subspace are sensitive to the distribution of roles you put in. The moderate cross-universe cosine similarities (~0.47 median between within-universe PC1 directions, but ranging from ~0.05 to ~0.95) are direct evidence: different character mixes yield different top directions, even when those directions encode semantically similar structure. Lu et al. show that for a fixed role set, the geometry is consistent across models --- our results suggest this cross-model consistency is a nontrivial finding, since changing the role distribution does change the geometry.

** Limitations

- All results are from Qwen3-32B at layer 32. Lu et al. show cross-model consistency for their axis. We haven't shown whether the results here generalize.
- Character instructions are minimal and were generated by Claude (e.g., "You are Harry Potter from Harry Potter. Respond as this character would."). Results may be sensitive to instruction quality or phrasing.
- The LLM feature discovery and rating pipeline uses Claude to evaluate model outputs. There could be shared biases between the evaluating LLM and the model being studied.
- Only 12 universes are analyzed; 241 of 1,268 characters are ungrouped and silently dropped from within-universe analyses.
- The feature regressions use 34--98 characters per universe (all non-discovery characters) with 6 predictors. Egyptian Mythology (n=34) has the fewest degrees of freedom.

* Appendix: Residual PC Feature Regressions

Residual PCs are within-universe PCA on activations after projecting out the role space. These directions are orthogonal to Lu et al.'s 275-role subspace by construction. The feature labels overlap with the within-universe and role PC labels (formality, warmth) for modern franchises, but are noisier and more heterogeneous for mythology and Shakespeare. Median adj. R² = 0.43, with a third of entries (8/24) below 0.3.
