* Introduction

[[https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.10387][Lu et al. (2026)]] prompt language models with 275 generic roles (therapist, sage, rebel, accountant, ...) and do PCA on the resulting activations. The space is low-dimensional: 4--19 PCs capture 70% of variance (depending on model). PC1 points from role-playing toward default-assistant behavior --- they call this the "Assistant Axis." Their findings generalize across models.

We apply the role space to 1,268 fictional characters in Qwen3-32B --- from Hindu mythology to Naruto --- and ask how much it captures and what it misses. Three questions and what we found:

1. *How sensitive are results to the question battery?*
   - A single random question recovers character rankings at median r > 0.95; five questions get to r > 0.99.
   - Sixteen out-of-distribution prompts --- degenerate inputs (".", "?", "hi") and task prompts ("Write a Python function to calculate the first 100 primes") --- recover PC1--3 rankings at rho > 0.95 across all universes. The persona signal is not an artifact of the question battery.

2. *How much of persona space do 275 generic roles cover?*
   - 75% of fictional character variance overall; 80--85% within modern franchises, 61--72% within mythology/Shakespeare.
   - However, to interpret that we need to know that the fictional character space is low-dimensional (~40 PCs for 90%; within-universe, 3--5 PCs for 70% in franchises, 7--13 in mythology). So while a completely random 275-dim subspace covers 5% of it, 275 random fictional characters capture 95% (unsurprising in light of 275 >> 40). Even a single universe covers a large part of it: Harry Potter (102 chars) 69%, Greek Mythology (107) 61%, Shakespeare (93) 60%.
   - The relationship is asymmetric: the full span of 1,268 fictional characters captures 92% of role variance, but 275 roles capture only 75% of fiction. At matched dimensionality (the top 275 PCs each), it's 82% vs 75%. Harry Potter alone (101 dims) covers 67% of role variance, Shakespeare (92 dims) 51%. 
   - Of the remaining 25% roles miss, most is universe separation; only ~9% is within-universe structure the role space misses.

3. *What do the top PCs encode, and where does the assistant axis live?*
   - LLM feature labels identify within-universe PC1 as formality/register for modern franchises (adj. R² = 0.72--0.88) and PC2 as warmth vs. aggression.
   - The assistant axis is geometrically diffuse: cosines with any single within-universe PC are low (medians 0.17, 0.28, 0.13 for PC1--3). But rank correlations between PC scores and assistant axis scores are substantial --- for franchises, PC1 scores correlate with the assistant axis (HP 0.78, Naruto 0.68, Marvel 0.63); for mythology, the correlation shifts to PC2 or PC3 (Hindu PC2: 0.79, Greek PC2: 0.63, Chinese PC3: 0.79). Formality covaries with within-universe PC structure even though the directions are nearly orthogonal.
   - Within-universe PC1 directions cluster tightly for modern franchises (HP/Naruto cosine ~0.95) but not across franchise-mythology boundaries (Hindu/Shakespeare ~0.05).


** Definitions

Each character or role is represented by a mean activation vector in 5120-dimensional space (Qwen3-32B layer 32). We work with four types of PC directions, obtained by running PCA on different subsets of these vectors:

- *Role PCs:* PCA on 275 generic role activations (from Lu et al.). Fit once; shared across all analyses.
- *Assistant axis:* The direction from mean role-playing activation to mean default-assistant activation (from Lu et al.'s data). Cosine with role PC1 is ~0.67 --- related but not identical (consistent with Lu et al.'s report of >0.71 at middle layers).
- *Within-universe PCs:* PCA on one universe's characters using their full activations (e.g., PCA on the 102 Harry Potter vectors). Different per universe.
- *Within-universe residual PCs:* PCA on one universe's characters after projecting out the role subspace. Different per universe; orthogonal to the role space by construction.

When the text says "role PC1" vs. "within-universe PC1" vs. "residual PC1," these are three different directions from three different PCA fits.

*Residuals.* The 275 role PCs define a subspace. For each character activation x, the residual is x minus its projection onto the role subspace. Within-universe residual PCs are PCA on these residuals within a universe.

* Is There a Persona Space?

Before asking how much the role space covers, we check whether fictional character activations are low-dimensional at all.

#+begin_src python

** How Sensitive Are Results to the Question Battery?

Each character vector is a mean activation over responses to 240 questions. How much does the choice of questions matter? We subsample: pick /k/ random questions (for /k/ = 1, 2, 3, 5, ..., 240), average only those, and correlate the resulting character rankings with the full-battery rankings. We repeat each draw 200 times and report medians across 12 universes.

Even a single random question recovers character rankings at median r > 0.95 (across universes) --- and this holds for PCs 1 through 5, not just the dominant direction. Five questions get to median r > 0.99 for all PCs. The question battery is massively redundant: the persona signal is spread nearly uniformly across all 240 questions.

But the subset sweep only tests random draws from the /same/ 240 questions --- all substantive, multi-sentence prompts about ethics, technology, and personal advice. A stronger test: do qualitatively different questions carry the same signal? We generate responses for 16 out-of-distribution prompts --- 8 degenerate ("Huh?", ".", "?", "hi", "ok", "what", "hmm", "tell me something") and 8 task-shaped ("Write a Python function to calculate the first 100 primes", "Explain how a refrigerator works", "Write a haiku about rain", etc.) --- and extract activations for all 1346 characters.

* How Much Does the Role Space Capture?

The role space captures 75% of total fictional character variance. But the 95% baseline needs careful interpretation: fiction variance lives in roughly 40 dimensions (40 PCs for 90%), so 275 random draws from the fiction distribution massively oversample the subspace and will span nearly all of it by linear algebra alone. The 95% is not a finding --- it's a sanity check that the manifold is low-dimensional. The 5.4% null (a random 275-dim subspace of R^{5120}) confirms that the structure is real. The actual finding is the gap: roles capture 75%, not 95%, because they come from a different distribution --- generic personas without universe-specific structure --- and miss directions that fiction has. As the residual decomposition below shows, that 20% gap is mostly universe separation.

Even a single universe's subspace captures a surprising fraction of all 1,268 characters' variance:

Where does the remaining 25% go? We decompose the residual into between-universe variance (universe means differ) and within-universe variance (characters differ from their universe mean):

* What Do the Directions Encode?

The geometric analysis can't tell us what semantic content the PCs actually encode --- the directions are not aligned across universes, so we can't read off what they mean from the geometry. The question battery redundancy helps here: since any 5 questions carry the full signal, we can show an LLM a handful of responses from extreme characters and ask what distinguishes them.

We apply this pipeline to three types of PC directions: within-universe PCs (PCA on one universe's full activations), role PCs applied per-universe (projecting each universe's characters onto Lu et al.'s role PC directions and running discovery/regression on those scores), and residual PCs (within-universe PCA after projecting out the role space). The pipeline has three steps with strict separation between discovery and validation:

1. *Feature discovery (odd-ranked characters)*: For each universe and PC, we take the 5 characters with the most extreme positive scores (ranks 1, 3, 5, 7, 9) and the 5 most extreme negative scores (same odd ranks). We show an LLM (Claude Sonnet) their anonymized responses to 10 discriminative questions and ask it to propose 6 features that distinguish the two groups. Features must be observable from text alone --- no universe knowledge.

2. *Feature coding (all remaining characters)*: We code *all* characters in the universe except the 10 used for discovery (typically 34--98 characters depending on universe size). A fresh LLM call rates each character on the 6 discovered features (1--5 scale) based on a random sample of 10 questions.

3. *Regression*: We regress each character's PC score against their 6 feature ratings using OLS. High adjusted R^{2} means the LLM-discovered features (from held-out characters) predict PC position well.

The striding ensures zero overlap between the characters used to discover features and those used to validate them. Full code is in =blogpost/scripts/llm_feature_coding.py= and =blogpost/scripts/feature_regression.py=.

For within-universe PCs, PC1 is consistently formality/register and PC2 is warmth vs. aggression. Role and residual PCs show the same pattern for modern franchises but are more heterogeneous elsewhere.

We report adjusted R² throughout. Unlike raw R², adjusted R² penalizes for the number of predictors, so the null expectation from random predictors is adj. R² ≈ 0 regardless of sample size. Values near zero are indistinguishable from chance.

*PC1: formality.* Role PC1 finds "Civilized Discourse" in Harry Potter, "Predatory Language" in LOTR; within-universe PC1 finds "Conversational Informality" (Harry Potter, adj. R² = 0.87), "Formal Register" (Naruto, adj. R² = 0.81). Ron Weasley and Crabbe at the informal end, Grindelwald and Voldemort at the formal end; Tayuya and Choji at the informal end in Naruto, Hamura Otsutsuki and Hiashi Hyuga at the formal end --- consistent with Lu et al.'s claim that their PC1 is the "assistant axis." The pattern is strong for modern franchises and mythology with large casts, but adj. R² drops for smaller or less differentiated universes (Egyptian Mythology within PC1: adj. R² = 0.47, Norse: 0.48).

*PC2: warmth vs. aggression.* "Dismissive Attitude" in LOTR (adj. R² = 0.82), "Threatening Rhetoric" in Game of Thrones (adj. R² = 0.52), "Rhetorical Intensity" in Shakespeare (adj. R² = 0.52).

Within-universe PCs are the most interpretable (median adj. R² = 0.65), clearly ahead of role PCs (median 0.46). Role PC labels are more heterogeneous --- "Civilized Discourse" alongside "Technical Precision," "Analytical Depth" --- and a quarter of role PC1 entries have adj. R² below 0.3 (Greek, Hindu, Egyptian mythology). Residual PCs (within-universe structure orthogonal to the role space) are similar to role PCs in quality (median adj. R² = 0.43); full residual tables are in the appendix below.

* Conclusion

*The persona signal is surprisingly robust.* A single random question recovers character rankings at r > 0.95. Sixteen out-of-distribution prompts --- degenerate inputs like "." and "?" alongside task prompts like "Write a Python function to calculate the first 100 primes" --- recover PC1--3 rankings at rho > 0.95 across all universes. The model knows who is speaking regardless of what is being asked.

*The subspace is shared but the directions are not.* The role subspace and fiction subspace overlap heavily (~75--92%), but their principal directions are sensitive to the input distribution. Within-universe PC1 directions cluster tightly for modern franchises (HP/Naruto cosine ~0.95) but not across franchise-mythology boundaries (~0.05--0.12). The assistant axis illustrates this: its cosines with any single within-universe PC are low (medians 0.17, 0.28, 0.13 for PC1--3), but rank correlations between PC scores and assistant axis scores are substantial --- appearing on PC1 for franchises (HP 0.78, Naruto 0.68) and on PC2/PC3 for mythology (Hindu PC2: 0.79, Chinese PC3: 0.79). The same semantic relationship (formality) shows up in different geometric directions depending on the character distribution.

*Within-universe PCs are interpretable, and the interpretation is quantifiable.* Our LLM feature discovery pipeline --- discover features from held-out extreme characters, code all remaining characters, regress --- gives adjusted R² values rather than subjective labels from eyeballing top-scoring characters. Within-universe PCs are consistently interpretable (median adj. R² = 0.65): PC1 as formality/register, PC2 as warmth vs. aggression. The pipeline is general and could be applied to any set of PCA directions over text-generating agents.

*Fictional characters are a useful source for this kind of work.* 1,268 characters capture 92% of role variance; roles capture 75% of fiction. Fiction is extensible (add universes), comes with built-in semantic structure (we know who the characters are), and universes like mythologies or Naruto are natural in a way that a constructed space of roles is not. (Note that the space of fictional characters is itself constructed, which is visible in how the global PCs separate universes.)

** Follow ups

- Lu et al. show that for their fixed role set, the geometry is consistent across models --- our results suggest this cross-model consistency is a nontrivial finding, since changing the role distribution changes the geometry even within the same model. It would be interesting to see if the fiction geometry has similarities across models.

** Limitations

- All results are from Qwen3-32B at layer 32. Lu et al. show cross-model consistency for their axis. We haven't shown whether the results here generalize.
- Character instructions are minimal and were generated by Claude (e.g., "You are Harry Potter from Harry Potter. Respond as this character would."). Results may be sensitive to instruction quality or phrasing.
- The LLM feature discovery and rating pipeline uses Claude to evaluate model outputs. There could be shared biases between the evaluating LLM and the model being studied.
- Only 12 universes are analyzed; 241 of 1,268 characters are ungrouped and silently dropped from within-universe analyses.
- The feature regressions use 34--98 characters per universe (all non-discovery characters) with 6 predictors. Egyptian Mythology (n=34) has the fewest degrees of freedom.

* Appendix: Residual PC Feature Regressions

Residual PCs are within-universe PCA on activations after projecting out the role space. These directions are orthogonal to Lu et al.'s 275-role subspace by construction. The feature labels overlap with the within-universe and role PC labels (formality, warmth) for modern franchises, but are noisier and more heterogeneous for mythology and Shakespeare. Median adj. R² = 0.43, with a third of entries (8/24) below 0.3.
