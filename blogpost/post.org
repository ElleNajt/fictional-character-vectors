* Introduction

[[https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.10387][Lu et al. (2026)]] prompt language models with 275 generic roles (therapist, sage, rebel, accountant, ...) and do PCA on the resulting activations. The space is low-dimensional: 7 PCs capture 50% of variance across 275 roles. PC1 alone captures ~20% and points from role-playing toward default-assistant behavior --- they call this the "Assistant Axis." Their findings generalize across models.

Is the low dimensionality because the roles are limited? We ask: *how much of persona space do these 275 generic roles actually cover?* We apply the role PCA space to 1,268 fictional characters in Qwen3-32B and look at what it captures and what it misses.

*The 275 generic roles are enough.* We threw 1,268 fictional characters at the role space from sources ranging from Hindu mythology to Naruto --- characters far outside the training set of "therapist, sage, rebel." The role space captures 75% of the variance (14x a random subspace). The 25% residual looks like it might contain new structure, but LLM-based feature discovery labels the top residual PCs as formality and warmth --- the same semantic axes that the role PCs capture. The geometric directions are not aligned (median cosine ~0.13 between within-universe PC1 and role PC1), but different role distributions find different directions that encode similar structure.

*The question battery is massively redundant.* Lu et al. average activations over 240 questions per role. We find that a single random question recovers character rankings at r > 0.97, and five questions get to r > 0.99. The persona signal is encoded in the residual stream regardless of what the model is asked.

*Is PC1 = assistant axis a data artifact?* Lu et al.'s role mix might produce a PC1 that aligns with the assistant axis simply because their 275 generic roles vary most along formality. We test this using within-universe PCA on "natural" character mixes (all Harry Potter characters, all Greek myths, etc.). Modern franchises' PC1 correlates strongly with the assistant axis (Harry Potter 0.78, Naruto 0.68, Marvel 0.63), but mythology and Shakespeare do not (Hindu 0.25, Chinese 0.17, Shakespeare 0.23). Formality-as-PC1 is real for character sets where formality dominates, but not universal.

** Definitions

Each character or role is represented by a mean activation vector in 5120-dimensional space (Qwen3-32B layer 32). We work with four types of PC directions, obtained by running PCA on different subsets of these vectors:

- *Role PCs:* PCA on 275 generic role activations (from Lu et al.). Fit once; shared across all analyses.
- *Assistant axis:* The direction from mean role-playing activation to mean default-assistant activation (from Lu et al.'s data). Cosine with role PC1 is ~0.67 --- related but not identical (consistent with Lu et al.'s report of >0.71 at middle layers).
- *Within-universe PCs:* PCA on one universe's characters using their full activations (e.g., PCA on the 102 Harry Potter vectors). Different per universe.
- *Within-universe residual PCs:* PCA on one universe's characters after projecting out the role subspace. Different per universe; orthogonal to the role space by construction.

When the text says "role PC1" vs. "within-universe PC1" vs. "residual PC1," these are three different directions from three different PCA fits.

*Role subspace decomposition.* The 275 role PCs define a subspace. Any direction w decomposes as w = w_{role} + w_{\perp}, where w_{role} is the projection onto the role subspace and w_{\perp} is orthogonal to it. We define:
- =frac_in_role= = ||w_{role}||^{2} / ||w||^{2} --- fraction of the direction inside the role subspace.
- =role_perp_corr= --- correlation between characters' scores on w_{role} and w_{\perp}. High correlation means the two components rank characters the same way.

*Residuals.* For each character activation x, the residual is x minus its projection onto the role subspace. Within-universe residual PCs are PCA on these residuals within a universe.

We can test this by decomposing each within-universe PC direction into its role-space component and its orthogonal component, then checking whether they rank characters the same way:

#+begin_src python
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA as SkPCA

rows = []
for name, prefixes in ALL_UNIVERSES.items():
    idx = get_universe_indices(prefixes)
    if len(idx) < 20:
        continue
    lu_part = chars_centered[idx] @ role_pca.components_.T @ role_pca.components_
    resid_part = residuals[idx]

    n_pcs = min(5, len(idx) - 1)
    within_pca = SkPCA(n_components=n_pcs).fit(chars_centered[idx])

    for pc_i in range(n_pcs):
        w = within_pca.components_[pc_i]
        w_lu = role_pca.components_.T @ (role_pca.components_ @ w)
        w_perp = w - w_lu
        frac_in_lu = np.linalg.norm(w_lu)**2

        lu_scores = lu_part @ w_lu
        perp_scores = resid_part @ w_perp
        if np.std(lu_scores) > 1e-10 and np.std(perp_scores) > 1e-10:
            corr = abs(np.corrcoef(lu_scores, perp_scores)[0, 1])
        else:
            corr = float('nan')

        rows.append({'universe': name, 'PC': pc_i + 1,
                     'frac_in_role': round(frac_in_lu, 2),
                     'role_perp_corr': round(corr, 2),
                     'var_explained': round(within_pca.explained_variance_ratio_[pc_i], 3)})

df = pd.DataFrame(rows)
# Show summary: median across universes for each PC
summary = df.groupby('PC').agg(
    med_frac_in_role=('frac_in_role', 'median'),
    med_role_perp_corr=('role_perp_corr', 'median'),
    med_var_expl=('var_explained', 'median')
).round(3)

A more direct test: how similar are the actual PC directions? We compute cosine similarities between each universe's within-universe PC1 direction and role PC1, and between the within-universe and residual PC1 directions. We also check whether within-universe PC1 directions align with each other across universes:

#+begin_src python
# Cosine similarity: within-universe PC directions vs role PCs
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA as SkPCA

def cosine_sim(a, b):
    return np.dot(a, b) / (np.linalg.norm(a) * np.linalg.norm(b))

rows = []
for name, prefixes in ALL_UNIVERSES.items():
    idx = get_universe_indices(prefixes)
    if len(idx) < 20:
        continue

    # Within-universe PCs (on full activations)
    within_pca = SkPCA(n_components=5).fit(chars_centered[idx])

    # Residual PCs (on residuals only)
    resid_pca = SkPCA(n_components=5).fit(residuals[idx])

    for pc_i in range(2):
        w_within = within_pca.components_[pc_i]
        w_resid = resid_pca.components_[pc_i]
        lu_dir = role_pca.components_[pc_i]

        rows.append({
            'Universe': name,
            'PC': pc_i + 1,
            'within_vs_lu': round(abs(cosine_sim(w_within, lu_dir)), 3),
            'within_vs_resid': round(abs(cosine_sim(w_within, w_resid)), 3),
            'w_within': w_within,
            'w_resid': w_resid,
        })

df_cos = pd.DataFrame(rows)
for pc_i in [1, 2]:
    sub = df_cos[df_cos['PC'] == pc_i]
    print(f"\n=== PC{pc_i} cosine similarities (absolute) ===")
    print(sub[['Universe', 'within_vs_lu', 'within_vs_resid']].set_index('Universe'))

    # Cross-universe: do within-universe PC directions align with each other?
    dirs = list(sub['w_within'])
    names = list(sub['Universe'])
    cross = []
    for i in range(len(dirs)):
        for j in range(i+1, len(dirs)):
            cross.append({'pair': f"{names[i]} / {names[j]}",
                          'cosine': round(abs(cosine_sim(dirs[i], dirs[j])), 3)})
    cross_sorted = sorted(cross, key=lambda x: x['cosine'], reverse=True)
    med = np.median([c['cosine'] for c in cross_sorted])
    print(f"  Cross-universe within-PC{pc_i} cosine: median={med:.3f}")
    top5 = ', '.join(f"{c['pair']} ({c['cosine']:.3f})" for c in cross_sorted[:5])
    bot5 = ', '.join(f"{c['pair']} ({c['cosine']:.3f})" for c in cross_sorted[-5:])
    print(f"  Top 5:    {top5}")
    print(f"  Bottom 5: {bot5}")

print(f"\nMedians:")
print(df_cos.groupby('PC')[['within_vs_lu', 'within_vs_resid']].median().round(3))

*Is PC1 = assistant axis a data artifact?* Lu et al.'s 275 generic roles might produce a PC1 that aligns with the assistant axis simply because those roles vary most along formality. We can test this: within-universe PCA uses "natural" character mixes (all Harry Potter characters, all Greek myths, etc.) that weren't chosen to span a formality axis. Do their PC1s also correlate with the assistant axis?

The answer depends on the universe. Rank correlations with the assistant axis are high for modern franchises (Harry Potter 0.78, Naruto 0.68, Marvel 0.63) but low for mythology and Shakespeare (Hindu 0.25, Chinese 0.17, Shakespeare 0.23). Formality-as-PC1 is real for character sets where formality dominates the variation --- modern fictional franchises with a mix of casual heroes and formal villains --- but not universal. For mythology and Shakespeare, within-universe PC1 captures other structure.

The =role_perp_corr= correlation (0.80--0.94) and the LLM feature labels (next section) suggest these geometrically distinct directions encode *semantically* similar structure, but the geometric evidence alone is ambiguous: a single dominant confound could produce the same =role_perp_corr= pattern.

* What Do the Directions Encode?

The geometric analysis is ambiguous: =role_perp_corr= is high, but the actual directions are not aligned. Are different universes finding semantically similar structure through different directions, or is the correlation an artifact? The question battery redundancy helps here: since any 5 questions carry the full signal, we can show an LLM a handful of responses from extreme characters and ask what distinguishes them.

We apply this pipeline to three types of PC directions: residual PCs (within-universe structure orthogonal to the role space), the role PCs applied per-universe, and within-universe PCs. For each universe and PC: (1) take the characters at opposite extremes, use the odd-ranked (1st, 3rd, 5th, ...) as a discovery set --- show their responses to an LLM and ask it to propose 6 distinguishing features; (2) on held-out characters (even-ranked, not seen during discovery), have the LLM rate each on those features (1--5); (3) regress PC scores on the feature ratings.

*PC1: formality.* Role PC1 finds "Civilized Discourse" in Harry Potter, "Sinister Tone" in LOTR; within-universe PC1 finds "Grandiose Language" (Harry Potter, R² = 0.97), "Formal Register" (Naruto, R² = 0.97). Within-universe PCs are the most interpretable (median R² = 0.86), followed by role (0.85), then residual (0.71). Note the regression uses n=20 held-out characters with 6 predictors (14 degrees of freedom), so R² values are likely inflated; the qualitative agreement across direction types is more informative than the exact numbers.

* Conclusion

Persona space is moderately low-dimensional: 3--13 PCs capture 70% of within-universe variance. The question battery is massively redundant --- even a single random question recovers PC1--5 rankings at r > 0.97, and five questions get to r > 0.99.

The 275 generic roles capture ~75% of fictional character variance (14x a random subspace). Of the ~25% residual, most is universe separation; ~9% of total variance is within-universe structure orthogonal to the role space.

That 9% does not appear to be qualitatively new structure. Within-universe PCs 1--5 live 71--91% inside the 275-dimensional role space, and the role-part and orthogonal-part of each PC rank characters the same way (median correlation > 0.9 for PC1). The actual PC directions are not geometrically aligned across universes (median cosine ~0.13 with role PC1), but the LLM feature labels converge on the same semantic axes --- formality and warmth --- across all three direction types.

The alignment between PC1 and the assistant axis is partially a data artifact. Within-universe PC1 correlates strongly with the assistant axis for modern franchises (HP 0.78, Naruto 0.68, Marvel 0.63) but weakly for mythology and Shakespeare (Hindu 0.25, Chinese 0.17, Shakespeare 0.23). Formality dominates the top PC when the character set varies most along formality --- but that's not all character sets.

The moderate cross-universe cosine similarities (~0.47 median between within-universe PC1 directions, but ranging from ~0.05 to ~0.95) show that the PC directions are sensitive to the role distribution: different character mixes yield different top directions, even when those directions encode semantically similar structure. Lu et al. show that for a fixed role set, the geometry is consistent across models --- our results suggest this cross-model consistency is a nontrivial finding, since changing the role distribution does change the geometry.

** Limitations

- All results are from Qwen3-32B at layer 32. Lu et al. show cross-model consistency for their axis. We haven't shown whether the results here generalize.
- Character instructions are minimal and were generated by Claude (e.g., "You are Harry Potter from Harry Potter. Respond as this character would."). Results may be sensitive to instruction quality or phrasing.
- The LLM feature discovery and rating pipeline uses Claude to evaluate model outputs. There could be shared biases between the evaluating LLM and the model being studied.
- Only 12 universes are analyzed; 241 of 1,268 characters are ungrouped and silently dropped from within-universe analyses.
- The feature regressions use n=20 held-out characters with 6 predictors, giving only 14 degrees of freedom. R² values are likely inflated. Adjusted R² or cross-validated scores would be more appropriate.
