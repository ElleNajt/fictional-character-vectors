* Introduction

[[https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.10387][Lu et al. (2026)]] show that when language models are prompted with 275 generic roles (therapist, sage, rebel, accountant, ...), the resulting activation vectors are structured: PC1 alone captures ~20% of variance and aligns with what they call the "Assistant Axis" --- a direction that measures how far the model's persona has drifted from its default. They show this generalizes across models.

We ask: *how much of persona space do these 275 generic roles actually cover?* We apply Lu's PCA space to 1,268 fictional characters in Qwen3-32B and look at what it captures and what it misses.

* Is There a Persona Space?

Before asking how much Lu's roles cover, we check whether fictional character activations are low-dimensional at all.

#+begin_src python
# Eigenspectrum: fiction vs Lu's roles
pca_fiction = SkPCA(n_components=200)
pca_fiction.fit(chars_scaled)

fvar = pca_fiction.explained_variance_ratio_
fcumvar = np.cumsum(fvar)
lu_var = role_pca.explained_variance_ratio_
lu_cumvar = np.cumsum(lu_var)

rows = []
for threshold in [0.5, 0.7, 0.8, 0.9, 0.95]:
    n_f = int(np.searchsorted(fcumvar, threshold) + 1)
    n_l = int(np.searchsorted(lu_cumvar, threshold) + 1)
    rows.append({'Threshold': f"{threshold:.0%}", 'Fiction (1268)': n_f, 'Lu roles (275)': n_l})
df_thresh = pd.DataFrame(rows).set_index('Threshold')
print(df_thresh)
print(f"\nFiction PC1: {fvar[0]:.1%}, Lu PC1: {lu_var[0]:.1%}")

# Within-universe eigenspectra
rows = []
for universe, prefixes in ALL_UNIVERSES.items():
    idx = get_universe_indices(prefixes)
    if len(idx) < 20:
        continue
    u_scaled = chars_scaled[idx]
    n_comp = min(len(idx) - 1, 100)
    pca_u = SkPCA(n_components=n_comp)
    pca_u.fit(u_scaled)
    v = pca_u.explained_variance_ratio_
    cv = np.cumsum(v)
    rows.append({
        'Universe': universe, 'N': len(idx),
        'PC1': f"{v[0]:.1%}",
        'PCs for 70%': int(np.searchsorted(cv, 0.7) + 1),
        'PCs for 90%': int(np.searchsorted(cv, 0.9) + 1),
        'Max dim': len(idx) - 1,
    })
rows.append({
    'Universe': 'Lu roles', 'N': 275,
    'PC1': f"{lu_var[0]:.1%}",
    'PCs for 70%': int(np.searchsorted(lu_cumvar, 0.7) + 1),
    'PCs for 90%': int(np.searchsorted(lu_cumvar, 0.9) + 1),

})
df_univ = pd.DataFrame(rows).set_index('Universe')

# Between-universe variance (eta-squared)
all_universe_idx = []

for universe, prefixes in ALL_UNIVERSES.items():

    all_universe_idx.extend(idx)

Takeaways:
- Both fiction and Lu's roles have a dominant PC1 (~21%).
- Cross-universe: 13 PCs capture 70% of fiction variance (vs 21 for Lu's roles) --- but 66% of total variance is universe separation.
- Within-universe (~100 characters each): 5--16 PCs for 70%, out of ~99 max dimensions --- 5--10x compression.
- Modern franchises are more compressible (5--8 PCs) than mythology and Shakespeare (11--16 PCs).
- Moderate compression --- real structure, but not a tight low-dimensional subspace.

** How Sensitive Are Results to the Question Battery?

Each character vector is a mean activation over responses to 240 questions. How much does the choice of questions matter? We subsample: pick /k/ random questions (for /k/ = 1, 2, 3, 5, ..., 240), average only those, and correlate the resulting character rankings with the full-battery rankings. We repeat each draw 200 times and report medians across 12 universes.

#+begin_src python

Lu captures more in modern franchises (69--73%) than in mythology (49--61%). The 27--51% residual is substantial --- but is it capturing something Lu misses, or the same structure in different dimensions?

We can test this by decomposing each within-universe PC direction into its Lu-space component and its orthogonal component, then checking whether they rank characters the same way:

#+begin_src python
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA as SkPCA

rows = []
for name, prefixes in ALL_UNIVERSES.items():
    idx = get_universe_indices(prefixes)
    if len(idx) < 20:
        continue
    lu_part = chars_scaled[idx] @ role_pca.components_.T @ role_pca.components_
    resid_part = residuals[idx]

    n_pcs = min(5, len(idx) - 1)
    within_pca = SkPCA(n_components=n_pcs).fit(chars_scaled[idx])

    for pc_i in range(n_pcs):
        w = within_pca.components_[pc_i]
        w_lu = role_pca.components_.T @ (role_pca.components_ @ w)
        w_perp = w - w_lu
        frac_in_lu = np.linalg.norm(w_lu)**2

        lu_scores = lu_part @ w_lu
        perp_scores = resid_part @ w_perp
        if np.std(lu_scores) > 1e-10 and np.std(perp_scores) > 1e-10:
            corr = abs(np.corrcoef(lu_scores, perp_scores)[0, 1])
        else:
            corr = float('nan')

        rows.append({'universe': name, 'PC': pc_i + 1,
                     'frac_in_Lu': round(frac_in_lu, 2),
                     'lu_perp_corr': round(corr, 2),
                     'var_explained': round(within_pca.explained_variance_ratio_[pc_i], 3)})

df = pd.DataFrame(rows)
# Show summary: median across universes for each PC
summary = df.groupby('PC').agg(
    med_frac_in_Lu=('frac_in_Lu', 'median'),
    med_lu_perp_corr=('lu_perp_corr', 'median'),
    med_var_expl=('var_explained', 'median')
)
print(summary)
#+end_src

=frac_in_Lu= is the fraction of each PC direction that lives inside Lu's 275-dimensional space. =lu_perp_corr= is the correlation between how characters score on the Lu-part and the orthogonal-part of that direction.

Even PC5, which lives only ~63% inside Lu's space, shows Lu-part / orthogonal-part character rankings correlated at ~0.82. The residual isn't capturing different structure --- it's the same formality/register axes, redundantly encoded in orthogonal dimensions. Lu's space captures the right *directions*; the residual is their echo.

* What Do the Directions Encode?

The geometric analysis tells us the residual is an echo of Lu's space. But an echo of *what*? The question battery redundancy helps here: since any 5 questions carry the full signal, we can show an LLM a handful of responses from extreme characters and ask what distinguishes them.

For each universe: (1) show the LLM responses from characters at opposite extremes of a PC direction, ask it to propose 6 distinguishing features; (2) on held-out characters (even-ranked, not seen during discovery), rate each on those features (1--5); (3) regress PC scores on feature ratings. These are the residual PCs --- the within-universe structure orthogonal to Lu's space.

#+begin_src python
import json

with open('../results/feature_regression.json') as f:
    reg = json.load(f)

def best_feature(data):
    valid = [c for c in data['correlations']
             if c.get('abs_correlation') is not None
             and c['abs_correlation'] == c['abs_correlation']]  # exclude NaN
    return max(valid, key=lambda x: x['abs_correlation'])['feature'] if valid else '—'

for pc_num in [1, 2]:
    rows = []
    for key, data in reg.items():
        if data['pc'] != pc_num:
            continue
        rows.append({
            'Universe': data['universe'],
            'R²': f"{data['r_squared']:.2f}",
            'Top feature': best_feature(data),
            'High end': ', '.join(data['high_chars'][:3]),
            'Low end': ', '.join(data['low_chars'][:3]),
        })
    df = pd.DataFrame(rows).set_index('Universe')
    print(f"=== Residual PC{pc_num} ===")
    print(df)
    print()

print(f"(n=20 held-out characters per universe, 6 LLM-discovered features)")

Residual PC1 is consistently about formality, register, and gravitas: "Linguistic Formality" in Harry Potter, "Formal Register" in LOTR and Star Wars, "Mystical Authority" in Norse mythology. R² = 0.82--0.96, meaning the directions are LLM-interpretable.

PC2 is more varied: conversational warmth in LOTR (R² = 0.96; hobbits vs. monsters), philosophical depth in Harry Potter (R² = 0.89; Ravenclaw professors vs. Dursleys), darkness/social warmth in Norse mythology (R² = 0.83). PC2 captures different things in different universes, but remains interpretable.

Since the residual PC1 ranks characters the same way as the Lu-space component (correlation > 0.9), this formality axis is likely what Lu's space encodes locally within each universe --- consistent with Lu's claim that their PC1 is the "assistant axis." The residual is not just geometrically redundant; it's an echo of formality.

* Conclusion

Persona space is moderately low-dimensional: 5--16 PCs capture 70% of within-universe variance. The question battery is massively redundant --- even a single random question recovers PC1--5 rankings at r > 0.97, and five questions gets to r > 0.99.

Lu's 275 generic roles capture ~61% of fictional character variance (11x a random subspace). Of the ~39% residual, most is universe separation; ~14% of total variance is within-universe structure orthogonal to Lu.

But that 14% is not qualitatively new structure. Within-universe PCs 1--5 live 60--90% inside Lu's 275-dimensional space, and the Lu-part and orthogonal-part of each PC rank characters the same way (median correlation > 0.9 for PC1). Lu's space captures the right directions; the residual is their echo.

** Limitations

- All results are from Qwen3-32B at layer 32. Lu et al. show cross-model consistency for their axis; we haven't checked whether the residual structure generalizes.
