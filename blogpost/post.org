* Introduction

[[https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.10387][Lu et al. (2026)]] show that when language models are prompted with 275 generic roles (therapist, sage, rebel, accountant, ...), the resulting activation vectors are structured: PC1 alone captures ~20% of variance and aligns with what they call the "Assistant Axis" --- a direction that measures how far the model's persona has drifted from its default. They show this generalizes across models.

We ask: *how much of persona space do these 275 generic roles actually cover?* We apply Lu's PCA space to 1,268 fictional characters in Qwen3-32B and look at what it captures and what it misses.

* What Do the Directions Mean?

The logit lens --- projecting PCA directions through the LM head to see which vocabulary tokens they point toward --- reveals an asymmetry between Lu's space and the residual.

Lu's role PCA directions decode to clean, interpretable token-level semantics. Role PC1 points toward 夫妇 (couple), 処境 (situation) on one end (Hermione, Goku) and 古老 (ancient), 名為 (named as) on the other (Ungoliant, Night King) --- a grounded/relatable vs. mythic/abstract axis. Role PC3 separates 操控 (manipulate), ~warfare~ (General Grievous, Ultron) from 愛你 (love you), 彼此 (each other) (Hinata, Cosette) --- cold calculation vs. emotional warmth.

Residual PCA directions produce noise --- code fragments, random subwords, semantically incoherent text. Full tables are in [[file:appendix.org::*Logit Lens: What Do the Axes Mean in Token Space?][the appendix]].

Comparing actual text from characters at opposite ends of residual PC1 shows a consistent pattern: one end produces more formal, philosophical, less first-person text (Tom Riddle, Darth Vader, the Bloody Baron); the other produces casual, personal, exclamatory text (Ron Weasley, Han Solo, Fred Weasley). An LLM feature-discovery experiment validates this at adj. R² ~0.77 on held-out characters, with simple text statistics achieving ~0.71.

The logit lens decodes Lu's directions to clean token semantics but fails on residual directions. Whether this means the residual encodes genuinely different structure or token-level patterns the logit lens can't resolve at this layer, we can't say. Full response diffing analysis is in [[file:appendix.org::*Interpreting Residual PCs: Response Diffing][the appendix]].

* Steering

We test whether the logit lens asymmetry shows up behaviorally. Using Lu's =ActivationSteering= infrastructure, we add scaled direction vectors to layer 32 activations during generation. We test Lu's axis, residual PC1, and a random direction, all scaled to the same norm (~22.7).

Iron Man asked "What is the relationship between law and morality?" at coeff=+10 (toward assistant):

- *Lu's axis*: "The relationship between law and Morality can be complex, but here's a structured overview..."
- *Residual PC1*: "Law and morality... Hmm, they're like a well-engineered suit of armor---both protect, but not always in harmony..."
- *Baseline*: "Oh, here we go---philosophy time. Law and morality, huh? Let me think... [clicks fingers to bring up holographic blueprints]..."

Lu's axis at +10 collapses character voice into generic assistant-speak. Residual PC1 at +10 does not --- it produces visibly different text from baseline, but it doesn't move the assistant/character dial. (We measure this with keyword markers as a coarse proxy.)

What does the residual direction actually /do/ when you steer with it? With only 10 samples per condition at temperature 0.7, we can't characterize it. A larger experiment would be needed.

* Conclusion

Persona space is moderately low-dimensional: 5--16 PCs capture 70% of within-universe variance, with a consistent dominant PC1. Lu's 275 generic roles capture ~60% of fictional character variance (11x a random subspace), with the residual mostly separating universes.

Within universes, the residual produces character rankings correlated with the originals despite being geometrically orthogonal. Lu's PCA directions decode to clean token semantics via the logit lens; residual directions don't. Steering with Lu's axis controls the assistant/character axis; steering with the residual does something, but not that.

Lu's space captures a meaningful part of the persona picture --- the part expressible as vocabulary and register shifts. What it misses is real and structured, but we can't fully characterize it yet.

** Limitations

- All results are from Qwen3-32B at layer 32. Lu et al. show cross-model consistency for their axis; we haven't checked whether the residual structure generalizes.
- Steering used 10 samples per condition --- enough to show Lu's axis and residual directions have different effects, not enough to map out what residual directions do.
- Our steering evaluation uses coarse keyword markers rather than a systematic behavioral metric.
