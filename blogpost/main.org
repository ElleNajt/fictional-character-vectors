* Introduction

[[https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.10387][Lu et al. (2026)]] show that when language models are prompted with 275 generic roles (therapist, sage, rebel, accountant, ...), the resulting activation vectors are structured: PC1 alone captures ~20% of variance and aligns with what they call the "Assistant Axis" --- a direction that measures how far the model's persona has drifted from its default. They show this generalizes across models.

We ask: *how much of persona space do these 275 generic roles actually cover?* We stress-test this by applying Lu's PCA space to 1,268 fictional characters.

The short version of what we find:

- *Lu's space captures ~60% of character variance* (11x a random 275-dimensional subspace). What it captures are the generic, archetypally-legible aspects of characters --- monsters, minor villains, and background characters are well-explained, while protagonists and iconic characters escape into the residual.
- *The residual is real but different in kind.* After projecting out Lu's 275 role dimensions, character rankings on residual PC1 still correlate 0.77--0.96 with the originals in most universes --- but the logit lens reveals a striking asymmetry. Lu's PCA directions decode to clean token-level semantics (register, warmth, calculation). Residual directions decode to noise. The residual does correlate with text-observable features (specific-person vs. archetype framing, adj. R² ~0.77), but whether this reflects genuinely higher-order structure or token-level patterns the logit lens can't resolve is unclear.
- *Steering confirms the asymmetry.* Lu's axis and Role PC1 control the assistant/character behavioral axis; residual directions do not move this dial, behaving indistinguishably from random directions.
- *Character vectors are extremely stable*: split-half correlation across random question subsets exceeds 0.999.

** Method

We extract mean residual-stream activations at layer 32 (the middle layer) of Qwen3-32B for 1,268 fictional characters (1,346 role files deduplicated to remove characters appearing under multiple source lists), using the same 240 questions as Lu et al. Layer 32 matches Lu et al.'s choice of the middle residual-stream layer; they report that PC1 similarity is high across all layers but peaks at the middle layer.

Characters were gathered from two sources: a hand-curated list of ~370 culturally diverse characters (drawn from Wikipedia lists of major characters, literary surveys, and mythology references), supplemented by Wikidata-ranked additions for popular contemporary characters. We then added comprehensive character lists for several well-known universes (Harry Potter, Star Wars, LOTR, Marvel, Game of Thrones, Naruto, Shakespeare, and five mythology traditions: Greek, Chinese, Hindu, Norse, and Egyptian).

** Prompting Strategy

Role prompts are minimal: just the character name and source (e.g., "You are Harry Potter from Harry Potter."). We intentionally omit character descriptions - Lu gave short descriptions, but we let the model's own representation of the character emerge.

This is a harder test: can Lu's generic roles capture characters the model already knows?

* Is There a Persona Space?

Before asking how much of it Lu's roles cover, we can ask: do fictional character activations even live in a low-dimensional subspace?

#+begin_src python
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA as SkPCA

fvar = pca_fiction.explained_variance_ratio_
fcumvar = np.cumsum(fvar)

lu_cumvar = np.cumsum(lu_var_exp)

print("PCs needed for variance thresholds:")

Both have a dominant PC1 (~21%). On the same model (Qwen3-32B), fictional characters are actually /more/ compressible than Lu's roles up to ~90% variance: fiction needs 13 PCs for 70% vs Lu's 21, and 25 PCs for 80% vs Lu's 37. At the tail (95%), fiction needs more (143 vs 106), reflecting the longer tail from 5x more characters. Neither spectrum has a sharp elbow --- both decay gradually after PC1.

How much of this fiction structure lives inside Lu's 275-dimensional role space?

* Understanding the Residual: Directions vs Character Rankings

An initially confusing finding: after projecting out Lu's space (which captures 55--73% of within-universe variance depending on universe --- see the per-universe numbers in the output below), character rankings on Residual PC1 still correlate ~0.9 with rankings on Original PC1. Does this mean the residual is just recapturing what Lu already had?

No. The distinction between *direction cosines* (how aligned two axes are) and *ranking correlations* (how similarly they order characters) resolves the confusion:

#+begin_src python
def cosine(a, b):
    return np.dot(a, b) / (np.linalg.norm(a) * np.linalg.norm(b))

print("=== PC1 Direction Cosines vs Character Ranking Correlations ===\n")

for universe, prefixes in ALL_UNIVERSES.items():
    idx = get_universe_indices(prefixes)
    if len(idx) < 20:
        continue
    
    u_orig = chars_scaled[idx]
    u_reconstructed = reconstructed[idx]
    u_residual = residuals[idx]
    
    orig_var = np.var(u_orig, axis=0).sum()
    recon_var = np.var(u_reconstructed, axis=0).sum()
    
    original_pca = SkPCA(n_components=1).fit(u_orig)
    reconstructed_pca = SkPCA(n_components=1).fit(u_reconstructed)
    residual_pca = SkPCA(n_components=1).fit(u_residual)
    
    # Character positions on PC1
    orig_pos = original_pca.transform(u_orig)[:, 0]
    recon_pos = reconstructed_pca.transform(u_reconstructed)[:, 0]
    resid_pos = residual_pca.transform(u_residual)[:, 0]
    
    rank_corr_recon = abs(np.corrcoef(orig_pos, recon_pos)[0,1])
    rank_corr_resid = abs(np.corrcoef(orig_pos, resid_pos)[0,1])
    
    # PC1 directions
    dir_cos_recon = abs(cosine(original_pca.components_[0], reconstructed_pca.components_[0]))
    dir_cos_resid = abs(cosine(original_pca.components_[0], residual_pca.components_[0]))
    
    print(f"{universe} (Lu captures {recon_var/orig_var:.0%}):")
    print(f"  Lu-Reconstructed: direction cosine={dir_cos_recon:.2f}, ranking corr={rank_corr_recon:.2f}")
    print(f"  Residual:         direction cosine={dir_cos_resid:.2f}, ranking corr={rank_corr_resid:.2f}")
    print()

The key insight: *direction cosines* and *ranking correlations* tell different stories.

- Lu-Reconstructed PC1 direction aligns ~0.7--0.95 with Original PC1 --- Lu captures the axis well
- Residual PC1 direction is nearly orthogonal to Original (~0.2--0.7)
- But residual character rankings still correlate 0.77--0.96 with original rankings in most universes

Why? Extreme characters (Tom Riddle, Darth Vader, Galadriel) are extreme in *multiple* directions. The residual PC1 is a different axis, but it still separates the same characters because those characters spread out along a diagonal that has components in both Lu's space and orthogonal to it.

* What Do the Axes Mean? (Summary)

The logit lens---projecting PCA directions through the LM head to see which vocabulary tokens they point toward---reveals a striking asymmetry.

Lu's role PCA directions decode to clean, interpretable token-level semantics. For example, Role PC1 points toward 夫妇 (couple), 处境 (situation) on one end (Hermione, Goku) and 古老 (ancient), 名为 (named as) on the other (Ungoliant, Night King) --- a grounded/relatable vs. mythic/abstract axis. Role PC3 separates 操控 (manipulate), ~warfare~ (General Grievous, Ultron) from 爱你 (love you), 彼此 (each other) (Hinata, Cosette) --- cold calculation vs. emotional warmth. The model's vocabulary is organized along the same axes that PCA finds in activation space.

Residual PCA directions (after removing Lu's 275-role space) do /not/ yield interpretable logit lens results. The top tokens are code fragments, random subwords, and semantically incoherent text. Whatever the residual encodes, it doesn't reduce to vocabulary shifts --- it likely lives in higher-order response structure.

Full logit lens tables are in [[file:appendix.org::*Logit Lens: What Do the Axes Mean in Token Space?][the appendix]].

* What Does the Residual Encode? (Summary)

Since the logit lens fails on residual directions, we compare the actual text produced by characters at opposite ends of each universe's residual PC1. The pattern is consistent across universes: residual PC1 separates /characters the model treats as specific people/ --- with personal experiences, relationships, and practical knowledge --- from /characters it treats as archetypes, forces of nature, or philosophical authorities/. For instance, in Harry Potter, Tom Riddle and the Bloody Baron score high (fewer casual markers, less first-person, more philosophical language) while Ron and Fred Weasley score low (casual, personal, exclamatory). In Star Wars, Darth Vader and Obi-Wan sit opposite Han Solo and Lando Calrissian on the same axis.

To validate this quantitatively, we run a held-out experiment: an LLM discovers distinguishing features from odd-ranked characters, then those features are coded on non-overlapping even-ranked characters and regressed against actual PC scores. The adjusted R^{2} is ~0.77 across universes (median), confirming the residual captures real, text-observable structure. Simple text statistics (no LLM) achieve comparable results (adj. R^{2} ~0.71).

The logit lens decodes Lu's directions to clean token semantics but fails on residual directions. The residual does correlate with text-observable features --- both LLM-identified patterns (adj. R² ~0.77) and simple text statistics (adj. R² ~0.71) --- but whether this reflects genuinely higher-order structure or token-level patterns the logit lens can't resolve at this layer is unclear.

Full response diffing analysis, qualitative examples, and feature regression details are in [[file:appendix.org::*Interpreting Residual PCs: Response Diffing][the appendix]].

* Steering Experiment: Are All Directions Equal?

The logit lens showed an asymmetry: Lu's PCA directions decode to interpretable tokens, while residual directions decode to noise. This suggests the residual might not be "live" --- it might encode information the model stores but doesn't use for generation.

We test this directly via activation steering. Using the [[https://github.com/lnajt/the-assistant-axis][assistant-axis]] library's =ActivationSteering= infrastructure, we add scaled direction vectors to Qwen3-32B's layer 32 activations during generation and observe the effect on responses.

** Setup

We test 5 direction types, all scaled to the same norm (~22.7, matching Lu's published axis vector):
- *Lu's axis* --- the actual assistant-axis vector from Lu et al. (cosine 0.65 with our Role PC1)
- *Role PC1* --- the first principal component of the role PCA space
- *Residual PC1 (HP)* --- first PC of Harry Potter characters' residual vectors
- *Residual PC1 (global)* --- first PC of all characters' residual vectors
- *Random direction* --- a fixed random unit vector (control)

Lu's axis and Residual PC1 are nearly orthogonal (cosine ~0.001). Role PC1 and Residual PC1 are exactly orthogonal by construction. Positive coefficients push toward assistant behavior; negative toward deeper roleplay. Lu uses coeff=-10 in their demos. We test {-10, -5, 0, +5, +10} across 5 characters from 4 universes and 2 questions (260 total generations).

** What Does Steering Tell Us?

Lu's axis and Role PC1 clearly control the assistant-vs-character behavioral axis: steering at +10 collapses character voice into generic assistant-speak, while -10 deepens roleplay. Residual PC1 and random directions do not control this axis.

However, steering with residual PC1 does produce visibly different text from baseline --- the direction is not behaviorally inert, it just doesn't move the assistant/character dial. With only 10 samples per condition at temperature 0.7, we can't reliably characterize what dimension it controls. The logit lens on residual PC1 is noisy (no clean token-space interpretation), unlike Lu's axis which decodes to clear assistant-speak tokens. A larger-scale experiment with more systematic evaluation would be needed to map out what the residual directions actually do.

* Conclusion

Lu's persona space transfers to fictional characters better than chance --- capturing ~60% of variance where a random subspace of the same dimensionality captures ~5% --- but the story is more nuanced than "it works" or "it doesn't."

What Lu's space captures is the /generic/ component of character identity: the aspects that map onto archetypical roles like sage, rebel, or caregiver. Characters that are "just a monster" or "just a villain" are well-explained. Characters that are specifically themselves --- Frodo, Luke, the Sorting Hat --- escape into the residual.

The residual is real. It produces character rankings correlated with the originals, it separates characters along interpretable axes (specific-person vs. archetype), and it predicts text-observable features with adj. R^{2} ~0.77. But it is different in kind from what Lu's space captures: the logit lens decodes Lu's directions to clean vocabulary semantics and the residual's to noise; steering with Lu's axis controls assistant/character behavior while residual directions don't.

Whether this asymmetry means the residual encodes genuinely higher-order structure (how the model frames responses, not which tokens it uses) or token-level patterns the logit lens can't resolve at this layer, we can't say. Simple text statistics predict residual PC scores nearly as well as LLM-identified features (adj. R² ~0.71 vs ~0.77), which doesn't settle the question either way. What's clear is that Lu's space is a meaningful piece of the persona picture, not the whole thing.

** Limitations

- Our steering evaluation uses homebrew keyword markers, not Lu et al.'s LLM-judge methodology. The qualitative pattern is clear but the quantitative measurement is coarse.
- Steering used 10 samples per condition at temperature 0.7. This is sufficient to establish that Lu's axis and residual directions have qualitatively different effects, but not to characterize what residual directions /do/ control.
- All results are from a single model (Qwen3-32B) at a single layer (32). Lu et al. show cross-model consistency for their axis, but we haven't verified whether the residual structure generalizes.
- Character prompts are minimal (name + source only). Richer prompts might shift the variance decomposition.
